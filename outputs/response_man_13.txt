Choosing what to study at university is a significant decision that depends on your interests, strengths, career goals, and personal values. Here are some recommendations and considerations to help you decide:

### 1. **Self-Assessment:**
   - **Interests:** What subjects or activities do you enjoy? Think about what you like to read, watch, or do in your free time.
   - **Strengths:** What are you naturally good at? This could be analytical skills, creativity, communication, problem-solving, etc.
   - **Career Goals:** Do you have a specific career in mind? Research what degrees are typically required for that career.
   - **Values:** What is important to you? Job security, high income, helping others, creativity, innovation, etc.

### 2. **Popular Fields of Study:**

   - **STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics):**
     - **Computer Science:** High demand, good salaries, and diverse career options in software development, AI, cybersecurity, etc.
     - **Engineering:** Various specializations like mechanical, electrical, civil, and chemical engineering. Good for problem-solvers who enjoy building and designing.
     - **Mathematics/Statistics:** Useful in finance, data science, and academia.
     - **Natural Sciences (Physics, Chemistry, Biology):** Good for research, healthcare, environmental science, and more.

   - **Business and Economics:**
     - **Business Administration:** Versatile degree with options in management, marketing, finance, and entrepreneurship.
     - **Finance/Economics:** Good for careers in banking, investment, consulting, and policy-making.
     - **Accounting:** Stable career path with opportunities in auditing, tax, and financial analysis.

   - **Healthcare:**
     - **Medicine:** Long and challenging path, but rewarding if you're passionate about healthcare.
     - **Nursing:** High demand and various specializations.
     - **Public Health:** Focuses on community health, epidemiology, and health policy.
     - **Pharmacy/Pharmacology:** Good for those interested in drugs and their effects.

   - **Social Sciences:**
     - **Psychology:** Useful in counseling, human resources, marketing, and research.
     - **Political Science/International Relations:** Good for careers in government, diplomacy, and NGOs.
     - **Sociology/Anthropology:** Useful in social work, research, and community development.

   - **Humanities:**
     - **Philosophy:** Develops critical thinking and can be useful in law, ethics, and academia.
     - **History:** Good for teaching, research, and careers in museums or archives.
     - **Literature/Linguistics:** Useful in writing, editing, teaching, and translation.

   - **Arts:**
     - **Fine Arts/Design:** For creative careers in graphic design, illustration, animation, etc.
     - **Performing Arts (Music, Theater):** Challenging field but rewarding if you're passionate.
     - **Film/Media Studies:** For careers in filmmaking, journalism, and media production.

   - **Law:**
     - **Law:** Requires further study after undergraduate, but can lead to careers in various legal fields.

   - **Education:**
     - **Education:** For those interested in teaching and educational administration.

### 3. **Emerging Fields:**
   - **Data Science/Analytics:** High demand across various industries.
   - **Artificial Intelligence/Machine Learning:** Cutting-edge field with many opportunities.
   - **Renewable Energy/Environmental Science:** Growing field with a focus on sustainability.
   - **Cybersecurity:** Increasingly important with the rise of digital threats.
   - **Biotechnology:** Combines biology and technology for innovations in healthcare and agriculture.

### 4. **Practical Considerations:**
   - **Job Market:** Research the job market and future demand for careers related to your field of study.
   - **Salary Expectations:** Consider the earning potential of different careers.
   - **Further Education:** Some careers require additional education (e.g., medicine, law, academia).
   - **Flexibility:** Some degrees offer more flexibility and a wider range of career options.

### 5. **Personal Advice:**
   - **Follow Your Passion:** You'll be more motivated and successful if you enjoy what you're studying.
   - **Combine Interests:** Sometimes combining two fields (e.g., computer science and biology for bioinformatics) can open unique opportunities.
   - **Gain Experience:** Internships, part-time jobs, and extracurricular activities can provide valuable experience and help you decide what you like.
   - **Seek Guidance:** Talk to career counselors, professionals in fields you're interested in, and alumni from your school.

### 6. **Alternative Paths:**
   - **Vocational Training:** If university doesn't appeal to you, consider vocational training or apprenticeships in skilled trades.
   - **Gap Year:** Taking a year off to work, travel, or volunteer can provide clarity on what you want to study.
   - **Online Courses/Certifications:** Platforms like Coursera, edX, and Udemy offer courses that can help you explore different fields before committing to a degree.

Ultimately, the best choice is one that aligns with your interests and goals. Don't be afraid to explore different options and change paths if needed. Many successful people have taken non-linear routes to their careers.
