### **1. Based on Career Prospects and Job Growth**
If you want a degree that leads to strong job opportunities, high salaries, or growth potential, consider these fields:

#### **STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, Mathematics)**
- **Computer Science / Software Engineering**
  - *Why?* High demand, high salaries, remote work opportunities, and versatility (tech is in every industry).
  - *Careers:* Software developer, data scientist, cybersecurity analyst, AI/machine learning engineer, product manager.
  - *Salary Range:* $80,000–$150,000+ (varies by role and location).

- **Engineering (Mechanical, Electrical, Civil, Chemical, Aerospace, etc.)**
  - *Why?* Engineering skills are always in demand, and engineers solve real-world problems.
  - *Careers:* Mechanical engineer, electrical engineer, civil engineer, aerospace engineer, petroleum engineer.
  - *Salary Range:* $70,000–$120,000+.

- **Data Science / Statistics / Mathematics**
  - *Why?* Data is the new oil. Companies need experts to analyze and interpret data.
  - *Careers:* Data scientist, data analyst, actuary, quantitative analyst.
  - *Salary Range:* $90,000–$140,000+.

- **Healthcare (Medicine, Nursing, Public Health, Biomedical Engineering)**
  - *Why?* Healthcare is recession-proof and growing rapidly, especially with an aging population.
  - *Careers:* Doctor, nurse, physician assistant, biomedical engineer, healthcare administrator.
  - *Salary Range:* $70,000–$200,000+ (varies widely by role).

- **Information Technology (IT) / Cybersecurity**
  - *Why?* Cybersecurity is one of the fastest-growing fields due to increasing cyber threats.
  - *Careers:* IT specialist, cybersecurity analyst, network architect, cloud engineer.
  - *Salary Range:* $80,000–$130,000+.

### **How to Choose?**
Here’s a step-by-step approach to help you decide:

1. **Self-Assessment:**
   - What subjects do you enjoy the most in school?
   - What are your strengths (e.g., math, writing, problem-solving, creativity)?
   - Do you prefer working with people, data, or things?
   - What kind of work environment do you thrive in (office, outdoors, lab, remote)?

2. **Research Careers:**
   - Look up job descriptions for careers that interest you.
   - Use resources like the [U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/) to explore growth and salary data.
   - Talk to professionals in fields you’re considering (LinkedIn is great for this).

3. **Consider Practical Factors:**
   - How long is the degree program? (e.g., Medicine takes 7+ years, while CS takes 4).
   - What’s the cost of the degree, and what’s the return on investment (ROI)?
   - Are there internships or co-op opportunities in the field?

4. **Try Before You Commit:**
   - Take introductory courses in fields you’re curious about.
   - Do internships, job shadowing, or volunteer work in potential careers.
   - Explore online courses (e.g., Coursera, edX) to test your interest.

5. **Think Long-Term:**
   - Where do you see yourself in 10 years? Does the degree align with that vision?
   - Is the field evolving? Will the skills be relevant in the future?

6. **Don’t Stress Over "Undecided":**
   - Many universities allow you to start as "undeclared" and explore before choosing a major.
   - Some majors (like Business, Liberal Arts, or General Studies) are broad and can keep options open.

### **Red Flags to Watch For**
- **Over-saturated fields:** Some fields (e.g., journalism, fine arts) have more graduates than jobs. Research job prospects carefully.
- **Low ROI degrees:** Some degrees are expensive but don’t lead to high-paying jobs. Check salary data before committing.
- **Passion vs. Practicality:** It’s great to follow your passion, but make sure there’s a viable career path (or be prepared to pivot).
- **Burnout risks:** Fields like medicine, law, or investment banking are rewarding but can be grueling. Consider work-life balance.

### **Next Steps**

2. **Talk to a career counselor:** Many high schools and universities offer career counseling services.

4. **Experiment:** Take a gap year to work, travel, or intern in different fields to gain clarity.
